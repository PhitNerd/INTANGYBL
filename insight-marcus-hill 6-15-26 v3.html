Behavioral Risk

He Was Trying to Engineer the Climate.

He Just Didn't Have an Instrument.

Ronald V. Edwards II

Founder, Intangybl

By now you've probably seen the story about Marcus Hill — the 29-year-old Atlanta mailroom worker who spent three months managing his own schedule based on 'vibrational alignment,' declared Monday mornings discontinued due to 'aggressive negativity,' and was eventually terminated after refusing a performance review on the grounds that he didn't 'participate in systems that don't recognize his aura.'

It's funny. Read it. I'll wait.

Now let me tell you what everyone missed.

The Thermostat

Buried in the list of Marcus's behaviors is one detail most people laughed at and scrolled past: he would stand near the office thermostat, whispering the word 'balance,' while adjusting it.

That's not a joke. That's a blueprint.

The thermostat is communal property with no official owner. Whoever controls it shapes the physical experience of everyone in the building — body temperature, comfort, alertness, irritability. And Marcus wasn't submitting a facilities request. He wasn't asking. He was claiming authorship over the environment while maintaining complete plausible deniability. Nobody writes you up for standing near a thermostat.

What Marcus was doing — underneath the absurdity — was attempting to engineer organizational climate from a position with zero formal authority.

His instincts weren't wrong. His methodology was noise.

What He Was Actually Running

Look at the full pattern of his behavior and you'll see it isn't random eccentricity. It's a coherent — if delusional — influence campaign:

  • Rename things to shift how people relate to them. Coffee breaks become 'healing intervals.' Meetings become 'spiritually optional.' Language shapes culture. Marcus knew this.
  • Control physical comfort. The thermostat move wasn't about temperature. It was about establishing that he was the one who adjusted things.
  • Claim visible real estate. The dramatic pause at the receptionist desk every morning wasn't social anxiety. It was a presence ritual — high-visibility, repeated, impossible to ignore.
  • Build a parallel authority structure. 'Shift Vibe Manager' isn't delusion. It's an attempt to insert a new node into the org chart through sheer repetition.

This is what organizational behavior researchers call bottom-up culture shaping. It happens constantly in every organization. Most of the people doing it are not as obvious as Marcus.

The Part That Should Concern You

Marcus got fired because he was bad at it. He was too visible, too theatrical, too easy to document.

The people doing this with actual skill don't get fired. They get promoted.

They learn which environments reward performative confidence. They read the room faster than the room reads them. They know when oversight is low, when peer observation creates cover, when deadline compression makes colleagues easier to influence. They don't whisper at thermostats — they walk into performance reviews knowing exactly what the person across the table needs to hear.

And most organizations have no instrument to detect the difference between someone genuinely aligned with institutional values and someone who has simply learned to perform alignment under observation while behaving entirely differently when the conditions change.

The question isn't whether your organization has a Marcus. It's whether it has someone doing exactly what Marcus was doing — and doing it well.

The Gap

What Marcus lacked wasn't instinct. He correctly identified that climate is engineerable. That the way things are named matters. That physical environment shapes behavior. That visibility creates authority. These are not fringe ideas — they are foundational findings in organizational psychology.

What he lacked was an instrument. A way to measure whether any of it was working. A framework for understanding which conditions actually activate which behaviors — in himself and in the people around him.

Without that, intuition becomes performance. And performance, without accountability, becomes Marcus Hill, standing at a thermostat, whispering 'balance' into the void.

The organizations that get ahead of this problem aren't the ones with better HR policies. They're the ones that can finally distinguish between a behavioral signature that holds under pressure and one that only appears when someone is watching.

That's the instrument Marcus needed. It's also the one most organizations don't have.

Yet.
