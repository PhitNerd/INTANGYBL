About

Intangybl was built by someone who understands behavioral risk from the inside. Not as a concept. As a condition.

Act 01

The Layoff

AVI-SPL was my first tech role — the result of a deliberate career pivot, not a casual entry. Which is what made the layoff land the way it did. It wasn't just losing a job. It was losing the proof point that the reinvention was working.

Most people treat a setback as a pause. They wait to reorient, let the result settle, need something to stabilize before they move. I noticed I didn't do that. The loss registered — and then the next question was already forming.

That distance — between what happens and how long it owns you — became the first real data point. I just didn't have a name for it yet.

Act 02

Per Scholas

Per Scholas gave me the technical foundation — security practice, governance frameworks, penetration testing, CompTIA Security+, NIST CSF. The architecture of how organizations actually think about risk.

But what the program really built was a vantage point. I started seeing the distance between what security frameworks measure and what actually produces exposure. The frameworks were precise. The human element was being estimated. Nobody seemed bothered by that.

I was bothered by it. That's a specific kind of friction. It either fades or it turns into something.

Act 03

The Rejection

I applied for an internship at Bentley Systems. The writing sample I submitted made a single argument: if you can build a behavioral model of a person, you can simulate exactly how they would be targeted — before an attacker does it for real.

I didn't get the internship.

I read the rejection and moved on faster than I expected to. Not because it didn't matter — because the idea in that document was already more interesting to me than the position had been. The door closed. The question stayed open.

That's the pattern, if you're paying attention. The outcome passes. What you actually care about doesn't.

Act 04

Intangybl Is Built

So I built it. Not as a pivot, not as a consolation — as the only logical next move once I understood what I was actually looking at.

The security awareness training industry asks: what do people know? I ask a different question: how are people structured to behave under the conditions that actually produce insider risk? Those are not the same question. The distance between them is where Intangybl lives.

The IBRI measures four dimensions of behavioral exposure. Three of them are stable — unchanged by what anyone learns in a training cycle. They shift with conditions. Pressure, authority, cognitive load, rationalization under stress. The things that move people toward decisions they would otherwise never make.

I understand those dimensions. Not just as a researcher. As someone who has watched them operate — in high-stakes situations, in setbacks, in the moments where identity either holds or it doesn't.

Veros exists because I kept asking what produces that difference. I haven't finished answering it.
